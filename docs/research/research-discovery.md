# Research & discovery intake for martylouis.com

**Marty Thierry has strong positioning instincts and real design credentials, but martylouis.com is currently a single-page landing site that leaves almost all of its persuasive power on the table.** The anti-agency headline ("Professional Web Design Without the Agency Overhead") is a solid differentiator, yet it's unsupported by case studies, testimonials, process details, or any content that would make a skeptical B2B buyer trust a solo practitioner enough to book that discovery call. The site's GitHub repo confirms it's "still in progress" — which means there's a blank canvas to build on. What follows is a full research-backed intake covering audience personas, messaging strategy, content opportunities, and competitive context to inform the buildout.

---

## 1. Current state of martylouis.com

The live site is a single-scroll landing page with no navigation, no subpages, and no blog. It features a professional headshot, the headline **"Professional Web Design Without the Agency Overhead,"** a subheadline emphasizing direct designer access, faster turnarounds, and predictable pricing, and a single CTA: "Book a Discovery Call." Ten portfolio screenshots are displayed as a visual gallery — clients include EvolvClaims.com (insurance tech), Wattsware.com (PR/media SaaS), Gulf Realty 30A (real estate), Manatee Air (HVAC), and several other Florida Gulf Coast businesses — but none link to case studies or include any context about the work.

What's notably absent: no About page, no Services page, no testimonials, no process explanation, no pricing details, no blog, no FAQ, and no lower-commitment conversion paths (newsletter, free resource, etc.). The tech stack (Astro, Tailwind CSS, Vercel) signals modern development fluency. External profiles reveal Marty has significant experience — UX/UI design, WordPress development, agency overflow work, SalesForce integrations, A/B testing with Optimizely — plus he co-hosts "The Freelance Dance" podcast. None of this appears on the site.

**The core gap**: Marty's headline promises differentiation from agencies, but the site provides zero evidence to support it. The "predictable pricing" claim has no pricing. The "trusted resource" aspiration has no resources. The portfolio shows visual work but no business outcomes. Every missing element is a conversion opportunity.

## 2. Audience and persona insights

### Decision-makers at software companies

The buying committee for UX/UI design services at software companies typically involves **2–3 people minimum**: the person who identifies the need (usually a Product Manager), the person who evaluates design quality (a Design Lead or Engineering Lead), and the person who approves budget (VP of Product, CTO, or at startups under 50 employees, the CEO/Founder directly). Forrester research indicates B2B purchasing decisions now involve 6–13 stakeholders at larger organizations, but for freelance design engagements, the circle is tighter.

Their pain points cluster around four themes. **Design bottlenecks** — the engineering team is shipping faster than the design team can support, creating feature backlogs. **Speed-to-market pressure** — product launches delayed because UX work is behind. **Quality inconsistency** — prior freelancers delivered attractive mockups that proved unusable in production. **Integration friction** — external designers who don't understand existing design systems, tech stacks, or user bases require excessive onboarding.

Common triggers for seeking external help include new product launches, post-funding investment in design, rapid scaling where engineering outpaces design capacity, product redesigns driven by poor user metrics, a key designer's departure, or entering a new platform (e.g., building a first mobile app).

### Buyers for professional website design

For businesses needing websites or redesigns, the buyer is almost always the **business owner** at companies under 20 employees, or the **marketing director** at companies with 20–100 employees. Their pain points are more emotional: an embarrassingly outdated site, a DIY template that looks unprofessional, a website that generates zero leads, and general overwhelm about where to start. Many have been burned before — **the "disappearing freelancer" is cited as the #1 fear** across multiple industry surveys. Content Snare found that nearly all their clients had previous bad experiences, often involving designers who went silent mid-project.

Budget expectations range from **$2,000–$15,000 for freelancer-built custom sites** and $5,000–$50,000+ for agency work. SMBs expect fixed-price proposals, clear milestones, no surprise charges, and post-launch support. They often compare freelancer quotes to the $0–$500 cost of DIY templates, requiring education on why custom strategic design commands a premium.

### What both audiences care about most when evaluating

The evaluation hierarchy, ranked by research consensus across Clutch reviews, hiring guides, and buyer surveys:

1. **Portfolio quality and relevance** — specifically, work in their industry or at similar complexity
2. **Communication and responsiveness** — rated as the single most important differentiator; the speed and thoughtfulness of initial replies is treated as a proxy for how the engagement will go
3. **Clear process and methodology** — structured phases (discovery → wireframes → design → development → launch) build confidence
4. **Proven business results** — case studies with metrics, not just visual showcases
5. **Client testimonials from named individuals** — social proof with real names and companies
6. **Pricing transparency** — clear understanding of what's included and what isn't
7. **Personality and cultural fit** — especially for ongoing relationships

**What creates doubt**: a designer's own website looking generic or unfinished, no references, unclear or hidden pricing, slow responses during the inquiry phase, and generic proposals that feel copy-pasted.

### Common objections and fears

For the **solo-designer-vs-agency** decision specifically, prospects worry about limited resources ("What if the project needs copywriting, SEO, and development too?"), no backup if the freelancer gets sick or overwhelmed, and less formal project management structure. The strongest counter-arguments — and the ones Marty should build messaging around — are that **solo designers cost roughly 50% less than agencies**, the client works directly with the senior person doing the work (no bait-and-switch with junior staff), and decision-making is faster with no internal committees.

### Channels and content formats

For software company decision-makers, the discovery path runs through **referrals first, then LinkedIn, Dribbble, and Clutch.co**. For SMB website buyers, it's **referrals first, then Google Search** ("web designer near me," "website redesign for [industry]"), followed by networking events and social media. Both segments respond most strongly to **detailed case studies** (the single highest-converting content format), followed by process breakdowns, before-and-after showcases, and educational guides. LinkedIn is the dominant B2B social platform — personal accounts consistently outperform company pages, and founder-led content dramatically outperforms brand content.

## 3. Content and messaging strategy

### Messaging angles that resonate with B2B buyers

The most effective framing for B2B design buyers is **outcome-driven and ROI-focused**, not aesthetic-focused. "I design websites that generate leads" outperforms "I create beautiful websites" every time. The core messaging architecture should work on three levels:

**Primary message**: You get agency-caliber strategic thinking with the directness, speed, and value of working with one senior designer — no layers, no markup, no telephone game.

**Supporting proof points**: Direct designer access (the person you meet is the person who does the work), faster turnarounds (no internal committee approvals), predictable pricing (transparent, fixed-fee structure), and business outcomes (conversion improvements, traffic growth, reduced bounce rates).

**Differentiation from agencies**: The strongest angle identified across the competitive landscape is making solo status a feature, not a limitation. Brendan Ho at saasdesigner.com explicitly warns prospects against agencies: "Most agencies pair you with a lower-cost, less-experienced designer while charging a premium." Marty's existing headline already moves in this direction — it needs supporting evidence to land.

The language shift required is from **design terminology to business terminology**. Instead of "UX audit," say "identify what's costing you customers." Instead of "wireframes and prototypes," say "a clear plan before any design work begins." Frame design as a business investment, not a creative expense.

### Blog topics to attract software company clients

Topics should demonstrate strategic product thinking, not just visual skill. The highest-value topics, organized by intent level:

**Bottom-of-funnel** (prospects actively looking to hire): "How to Evaluate a UX Designer for Your SaaS Product," "Freelance Designer vs. Agency: Which Is Right for Your Software Product?", "What to Expect From a UX Engagement — Timeline, Deliverables, and Process."

**Middle-of-funnel** (problem-aware, exploring solutions): "7 Signs Your SaaS Product Has a UX Debt Problem," "Why Your Users Aren't Adopting Your Best Features," "The True Cost of a Confusing UI: Support Tickets, Churn, and Lost Revenue," "How to Reduce SaaS Churn Through Better Onboarding UX."

**Top-of-funnel** (building awareness and authority): "The Anatomy of a High-Converting SaaS Pricing Page," "Designing for Product-Led Growth: UX Patterns That Convert Free Users to Paid," "Accessibility Compliance for SaaS in 2026: What the European Accessibility Act Means for Your Product," "AI in Your Product UI: Designing Around AI Features Without Overwhelming Users."

### Blog topics to attract website design/redesign buyers

These should reverse-engineer the questions buyers ask before hiring. Research identified the top pre-purchase questions, each of which becomes a blog post:

**Highest-priority topics**: "How Much Does a Website Redesign Actually Cost? A Transparent 2026 Guide," "How Do I Know If My Website Needs a Redesign? 7 Warning Signs," "Will a Website Redesign Hurt My Google Rankings? How to Protect Your SEO," "Custom Design vs. Templates: What's the Real Difference for Your Business?" and "What to Expect During a Website Redesign: A Step-by-Step Timeline."

**Trust-building educational content**: "What Makes a Website Convert: The Principles Behind High-Performing Sites," "A Business Owner's Guide to Website Speed and Core Web Vitals," "The Anatomy of a High-Performing Homepage," and "DIY vs. Professional Web Design: An Honest Comparison."

**Seasonal triggers**: Q1 budget-planning content ("Planning Your Website Investment for the New Year"), Q3 mid-year audits ("Is Your Website Still Working for You?"), and Q4 holiday prep ("Is Your Site Ready for Holiday Traffic?").

### What "trusted resource" positioning looks like in practice

The research consensus is clear: **give away the "what" and "why," sell the execution.** Trusted-resource positioning means every piece of content should leave the reader smarter regardless of whether they hire Marty. The paradox works in his favor — people who can implement the advice will DIY (and may return later when they scale), while people who realize it's complex will hire him immediately. Big Cat Creative confirmed this dynamic: "Some people will use the post and create a DIY version, others will realize it's too much work and book your services."

Practically, this means publishing comprehensive guides with real frameworks (not gated teasers), being transparently opinionated about design best practices, showing work publicly through process breakdowns, addressing objections proactively before prospects even ask, and including soft CTAs ("If this feels overwhelming, I do this for a living — let's talk") rather than aggressive sales pitches. The recommended ratio is **80% pure value, 20% gentle self-promotion** woven naturally into the content.

The gold-standard examples of this approach: **Built for Mars** (Peter Ramsey), who publishes deep UX teardowns of major products that even non-designers find valuable; **Baymard Institute**, which built its entire reputation on freely available e-commerce UX research; and **Julie Zhuo**, whose honest blog about design leadership at Facebook became her personal brand.

### Content gaps and opportunities specific to martylouis.com

The most critical gaps, ranked by impact:

1. **Zero case studies with business outcomes.** The ten portfolio screenshots need to become full case studies showing problem → process → measurable results. This is the single highest-converting content type for B2B designer websites, and it's completely absent.

2. **No content targeting buyers, only designers.** The #1 mistake identified across the research — most designer blogs write for other designers (tutorials, tool reviews). Marty's content should target the people who *hire* designers: founders, marketing directors, product managers.

3. **No SEO foundation.** With one indexed page and no blog, the site has essentially no organic search presence. High-intent buyer keywords like "how much does a website redesign cost" and "freelance designer vs agency" are readily available.

4. **No pricing transparency.** The headline promises "predictable pricing" but shows none. Even publishing ranges ($X–$Y depending on scope) would be a powerful trust signal — research shows it's one of the most-searched topics.

5. **No lower-commitment entry points.** The only CTA is "Book a Discovery Call," which is a high-commitment first step. Adding a newsletter, free resource (website redesign checklist, UX audit template), or educational email sequence would capture prospects who aren't ready to call.

## 4. Competitive landscape and what's working

### How B2B-focused designer brands position themselves

Eight competitors were analyzed in detail, revealing consistent patterns. **The most successful B2B designer brands use niche exclusion as positioning** — they publicly turn away non-ideal clients. Eleken (eleken.co) states bluntly: "If you are not SaaS — don't apply." Brendan Ho at saasdesigner.com made his domain name a positioning statement. UITOP declares: "We work exclusively with B2B software." This counterintuitive narrowing builds credibility and commands higher fees.

**Business-outcome language dominates over design language.** Successful competitors talk about conversion rates, adoption metrics, and revenue impact — not aesthetics. Fuzzy Math leads with "2x Sales Conversion, +20% SaaS Lead Conversion." Donux highlights taking "core feature from 27% usage to 66%." The framing is consistently "strategic partner" rather than "vendor" — language like "dedicated designer on your side" and "design partner for teams that ship fast."

The most effective competitive sites share several structural elements: industry-specific landing pages (fintech, healthcare, SaaS), **quantified case studies front and center**, detailed process documentation, named testimonials from C-suite executives, and some form of risk-reducing entry point (Eleken offers a 3-day trial with real work; Donux offers a free UX audit).

### Content strategies driving results

The competitors winning on content follow one of two models. The **high-volume SEO approach** (Eleken, Azuro Digital) publishes multiple posts per week on niche design topics, building an organic traffic engine — Eleken has 100+ articles organized by SaaS design subtopics. The **low-frequency, high-quality approach** publishes 1–2 definitive guides per month (5,000+ words) targeting high-intent keywords. Both models work; the key is consistency.

Several competitors have built **proprietary tools as lead magnets**: Donux created Roastit (a UX audit product), Azuro Digital offers a free SEO checker and accessibility statement generator, and Fuzzy Math publishes a UX Glossary. These tools demonstrate expertise, generate inbound leads, and differentiate from competitors who only offer services.

The distribution pattern is remarkably consistent: blog content → repurposed into LinkedIn posts → newsletter → social snippets. **LinkedIn is the dominant channel** — personal founder accounts dramatically outperform company pages, and the most effective content types are multi-image posts, text-based personal stories, and polls.

### Where martylouis.com sits relative to the field

Marty's current positioning — anti-agency, direct access, predictable pricing — is directionally strong and aligned with what the best competitors do. But the execution gap is significant. Competitors like SaaS Designer back up their anti-agency stance with detailed explanations, case studies, and testimonials. Marty's site makes the claim in a headline but provides no supporting evidence.

The portfolio mix (local service businesses, real estate, HVAC, plus some tech clients like EvolvClaims and Wattsware) suggests a generalist practice. The competitive research shows that **the highest-performing solo designers specialize aggressively** — by industry vertical (SaaS only), product type (B2B dashboards), company stage (startups to growth), or engagement model (embedded designer). Marty doesn't need to narrow to one niche immediately, but the content and messaging should signal deeper expertise in specific verticals rather than positioning as a generalist.

## 5. Strategic recommendations and priority actions

The research points to a clear buildout sequence. **Phase 1 (immediate, highest impact)**: Add 3–5 case studies with business outcomes from existing clients — even retroactively gathering metrics from past projects. Build an About page that tells Marty's story, credentials, and philosophy. Create a Services page with process details and pricing ranges. Add testimonials. These foundational trust elements are table stakes that every competitor has.

**Phase 2 (content engine)**: Launch the blog with buyer-focused content, starting with bottom-of-funnel posts that target high-intent search queries ("how much does a website redesign cost," "freelancer vs. agency for website design"). Publish 2–4 posts per month. Create one downloadable resource (website redesign checklist or UX audit template) as a lower-commitment conversion path.

**Phase 3 (authority building)**: Begin LinkedIn content distribution repurposing blog posts. Develop industry-specific landing pages for the strongest verticals (SaaS/software and professional services are natural fits given existing clients). Consider a free tool or audit offering as a lead magnet, following the Donux and Azuro Digital playbook.

**The single most important shift** is moving from "here's what I do" to "here's what it means for your business." Every page, post, and case study should answer the buyer's core question: *"Will this person solve my problem and be reliable?"* The "trusted resource" positioning is the right instinct — it now needs content to make it real.
